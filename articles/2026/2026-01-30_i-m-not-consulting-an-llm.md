# I'm not consulting an LLM

## Core ideas (structured)

- The author argues against relying on GPT/LLMs for information-seeking because even a hypothetically perfect answer-delivery system would skip the formative part of research.
- Central comparison: an always-correct version of Google’s “I’m Feeling Lucky” would optimize for getting to an answer, but would remove the exploratory process through which people sharpen judgment.
- The author sees research as valuable not only for retrieving facts but for encountering contradictions, bad arguments, strange subcultures, dead ends, and other material that forces model-building.
- In the author’s framing, intellect develops through exposure to uncertainty and conflict, not through frictionless answer consumption.
- Returning from the thought experiment to real LLMs, the author argues that current systems are far from perfectly reliable and usually appear strongest only in domains where the user lacks expertise.
- The author’s core critique is epistemic: LLM outputs are plausible-sounding responses without guarantees that they are the best, most contested, or even correct answer.

## What seems to work (per the author)

- The one explicit exception is using LLMs for “stupid tasks”: repetitive, easily automatable procedures the author says they would previously have handled with an Emacs macro.
- For learning and inquiry, the author implicitly favors doing the underlying research process yourself rather than delegating it to a model.
- The author treats exposure to disagreement, contradictory sources, and uncertain footing as part of what makes research intellectually productive.

## Limitations / risks (per the author)

- Over-reliance on LLMs for finding information can strip away the experience of research, which the author sees as the mechanism by which intellect is trained.
- Smooth, confident responses can hide uncertainty and make weak or mistaken answers feel more solid than they are.
- In specialized domains, the author says LLM answers have not met the standard they would expect from a real expert.
- The author warns that plausible answers can prevent users from noticing hidden assumptions, disagreements, and failure conditions.

## Claims worth validating

- The claim that current LLMs only seem impressive in areas users do not understand well is presented as an extension of Gell-Mann amnesia, but is not substantiated here.
- The claim that the author has never received a sufficiently expert answer from GPT in a domain they know well is anecdotal and based on personal experience.
- The claim that LLMs are “intellectually corrosive” because they hide uncertainty is a strong normative judgment, not an empirical result in this post.

## Notes (your interpretation, clearly labeled)

- This piece is less about software-development productivity than about epistemic cost: it argues that answer compression can reduce the learning and judgment-building that happen during exploratory research.
- It is a useful counterpoint to workflow posts in this repo that focus on efficiency gains; here the author treats efficiency itself as part of the risk when the task is understanding rather than execution.
